This page illustrates how the auction works and how profits are calculated. In this example, there are three bidders (Bidder A, Bidder B and Bidder C) each with a demand for three units who are competing in market in which there are eight units of the same good up for sale.

Suppose Bidder A recieves randomly assigned values of $63, $60 and $44, Bidder B recieves randomly assigned values of $56, $54 and $41, and Bidder C recieves randomly assigned values of $59, $57 and $33.

Now suppose Bidder A bids $58 for the first unit, $52 for the second unit and $28 for the third unit she demands. Similarily, Bidder B bids $50 for the first unit, $42 for the second unit and $32 for the third unit she demands. Finally, Bidder C bids $51 for the first unit, $45 for the second unit and $30 for the third unit she demands.

Once all the bids are submitted, the computer will sort the bids from highest to lowerst. As you see in the table below, the eight highest bids have won the units. The ninth highest bid lost the item and is highlighted in pink. All bidders that won an item will have to pay their bid for that unit won.

In the example depicted in Table 1, bidder A earned 2 units while Bidder B and Bidder C earned 3 units. Bidder A’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: $63 - $58 = $5
  • Unit 2: $60 - $52 = $8
  • and nothing on unit 3
  • Total profits = $13

Bidder B’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: $56 - $50 = $6
  • Unit 2: $54 - $42 = $12
  • Unit 3: $41 - $32 = $9
  • Total profits = $27

Bidder C’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: $59 - $51 = $8
  • Unit 2: $57 - $45 = $12
  • Unit 3: $33 - $30 = $3
  • Total profits = $23
