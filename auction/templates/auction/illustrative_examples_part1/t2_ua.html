This page illustrate how the auction works and how profits are calculated. In this example, there are three bidders (Bidder A, Bidder B and Bidder C) each with a demand for three units who are competing against eachother in a market in which there are eight units of the same good up for sale.

Suppose Bidder A recieves randomly assigned values of $63, $60 and $44, Bidder B recieves randomly assigned values of $56, $54 and $41, and Bidder C recieves randomly assigned values of $59, $57 and $33 .

Now suppose Bidder A bids $63 for the first unit, $60 for the second unit and $20 for the third unit she demands. Similarily, Bidder B bids $56 for the first unit, $54 for the second unit and $40 for the third unit she demand. Finally, Bidder C bids $59 for the first unit, $57 for the second unit and $27 for the third unit she demand.

Once all the bids are submitted, the computer will sort the bids from highest to lowerst. As you see in the table below, the eight highest bids have won the units. The ninth highest bid lost the item. The ninth highest bid is highlighted in pink and sets the market price that the winning bidders will have to pay for the units won.

In the example depicted in Table 1, Bidder A earned 2 units while Bidder B and bidder C earned 3 units. Bidder A’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: $63 - $20 = $43
  • Unit 2: $60 - $20 = $40
  • and nothing on unit 3
  • Total profits = $83

Bidder B’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: $56 - $20 = $36
  • Unit 2: $54 - $20 = $34
  • Unit 3: $41 - $20 = $21
  • Total profits = $91

Bidder C’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: $59 - $20 = $39
  • Unit 2: $57 - $20 = $37
  • Unit 3: $33 - $20 = $13
  • Total profits = $89
