First, we focus on Table 1. Note bids have been sorted from highest to lowest, values are shown next to the bids. The bid that determines the market price (the 9th highest bid) has been highlighted with a pink background. The 8 highest bids above the pink background each earn an item. Note, the clearing price is always set by the only losing bid. In the example depicted in Table 1, bidder A earned 2 unit while bidder B and C both earned 3 units. Thus in the case of Table 1, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: $63 - $20 = $43
  • Unit 2: $60 - $20 = $40
  • and nothing on unit 3
  • Total profit = $83

In this example, Bidder A could have earned a third unit by bidding more, provided that bid was sufficiently high to edge out Bidder C. Table 2 illustrates the case in which Bidder A changes her bid on her third unit from $20 to $40; and all other bids are held constant. Focusing on Table 2, we see that the clearing price is now $27. Bidder A wins 3 units and bidder B wins 3 units while bidder C now only wins 2 and her losing bid sets the clearing price. Thus in the case of Table 2, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: $63 - $27 = $36
  • Unit 2: $60 - $27 = $33
  • Units 3: $44 - $27 = $17
  • Total profits = $86

In comparing the auction outcomes in Table 1 and Table 2, we see that Bidder A was able to increase her profits by bidding more on her third unit in order to win 3 units rather than 2. However, it is important to point out in this example that in earning 3 (rather than 2) unit, Bidder A caused the market clearing price to increase from $20 to $27. This directly affects the price that Bidder A had to pay for her two higher valued unit. In the scenario represented in Table 1 and Table 2, the increase in clearing price was small enough such that winning two units rather than one unit increased total profits. It should be noted that this will not always be the case.
