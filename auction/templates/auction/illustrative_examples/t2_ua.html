The following examples illustrate the uniform pricing rule and how profits are calculated. Each example will be illustrated from the perspective of bidder A who is competing against two rival bidders B and C. Each of the three bidders with demand for three units are competing in one auction in which there are eight units of the same good to be auctioned.

Example 1. First, we focus on Table 1 in Example 1. Note bids have been sorted from highest to lowest, values are shown next to the bids. The bid that determines the market price (the 9th highest bid) has been highlighted with a pink background. The 8 highest bids above the pink background each earn an item. Note, the clearing price is always set by the only losing bid. In the example depicted in Table 1, bidder A earned 2 unit while bidder B and C both earned 3 units. Thus in the case of Table 1, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: 63 - 20 = 43
  • Unit 2: 60 - 20 = 40
  • and nothing on unit 3
  • Total profit = 83

In this example, Bidder A could have earned a third unit by bidding more, provided that bid was sufficiently high to edge out Bidder C. Table 2 illustrates the case in which Bidder A changes her bid on her third unit from 20 to 40; and all other bids are held constant. Focusing on Table 2, we see that the clearing price is now 27. Bidder A wins 3 units and bidder B wins 3 units while bidder C now only wins 2 and her losing bid sets the clearing price. Thus in the case of Table 2, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: 63 - 27 = 36
  • Unit 2: 60 - 27 = 33
  • Units 3: 44 - 27 = 17
  • Total profits = 86

In comparing the auction outcomes in Table 1 and Table 2, we see that Bidder A was able to increase her profits by bidding more on her third unit in order to win 3 units rather than 2. However, it is important to point out in this example that in earning 3 (rather than 2) unit, Bidder A caused the market clearing price to increase from 20 to 27. This directly affects the price that Bidder A had to pay for her two higher valued unit. In the scenario represented in Table 1 and Table 2, the increase in clearing price was small enough such that winning two units rather than one unit increased total profits. It should be noted that this will not always be the case.

Focusing on Table 3, we see that Bidder A earned 2 unit while bidder B and C both earned 3 units. Profits for bidder A on this unit are calculated as follows: the valuation of the unit less the market price.

Thus in the case of Table 3, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as:

  • Unit 1: 63 - 20 = 43
  • Unit 2 : 60 - 20 = 40
  • and nothing on unit 3
  • Total profits = 83

As in the previous example, we are interested to see if Bidder A could increase her third unit bids in order to win 3 (rather than 2) unit. Table 4 illustrates this case when Bidder A changes her bid from 20 to 40 on her lower valued unit while keeping her bid on the higher valued units constant as well as all bids from Bidder B and C constant. This change in bidding strategy resulted in the clearing price now being set by Bidder C’s bid of 39. Bidder A wins 3 units and Bidder B wins 3 units while bidder C now only wins 2 and her losing bid sets the clearing price. Thus in the case of Table 4, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as:

  • Unit 1: 63 - 39 = 24
  • Unit 2 : 60 - 39 = 21
  • Unit 3: 44 - 39 = 5
  • Total profits = 50

Consequently, total profits are lower in Table 4 where Bidder A won 3 units relative to Table 3 where Bidder A won only 2 unit.

To summarize, examples 1 and 2 illustrated the effect of Bidder A using different strategies on total profits. As was the case in example 1, Bidder A earned more profit when she won 3 units compared to the case when she won 2 unit. However, example 2 illustrates another scenario when Bidder A actually earned less revenue when she won 3 rather than 2 unit. Underlying these two illustrative examples is the fact that winning 3 units instead of 2 unit will almost surely increase the clearing price and thus directly reducing the profits you can earn on the first unit. The net result is that in some cases it will be profitable to increase your bid on the third unit (example 1) and in some cases it will not be profitable to increase your bid on the third unit (example 2).
