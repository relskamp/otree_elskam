The following examples illustrate the pay-your-bid pricing rule and how profits are calculated. Each example will be illustrated from the perspective of bidder A who is competing against two rival bidders B and C. Each of the three bidders with demand for three units are competing in one auction in which there are eight units of a homogeneous good to be auctioned.

Example 1. First, we focus on Table 1 in Example 1. Note bids have been sorted from highest to lowest, values are shown next to the bids. The 9th losing bid has been highlighted with a pink background. The 8 highest bids above this pink background each earn an item. In the example depicted in Table 1, Bidder A earned 2 unit while bidder B and C both earned 3 units. Thus in the case of Table 1, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: 7.3 - 7 = 0.3
  • Unit 2: 6.3 - 5.8 = 0.5
  • and nothing on unit 3
  • Total profit = 0.8

In this example, Bidder A could have earned a third unit by bidding more, provided that bid was sufficiently high to edge out Bidder C. Table 2 illustrates the case in which Bidder A changes her bid on her third unit from 2 to 4; and all other bids are held constant. Focusing on Table 2, we see that Bidder A wins 3 units and bidder B wins 3 units while bidder C now only wins 2. Thus in the case of Table 2, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: 7.3 - 7 = 0.3
  • Unit 2: 6.3 - 5.8 = 0.5
  • Units 3: 4.4 - 4.0 = 0.4
  • Total profits = 1.2

In comparing the auction outcomes in Table 1 and Table 2, we see that Bidder A was able to increase her profits by bidding more on her third unit in order to win 3 units rather than 2.

Example 2. The next set of examples illustrates an alternative scenario in which Bidder A bids lower on her first highest valued unit in order to win 3 units while earning more profits.

Focusing on Table 3, we see that Bidder A earned 3 unit while bidder B earned 3 units and Bidder C won only 2 units. Profits for bidder A on this unit are calculated as follows: the valuation of the unit less the market price.

Thus in the case of Table 3, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as:

  • Unit 1: 7.3 - 7.0 = 0.3
  • Unit 2 : 6.0 - 5.8 = 0.2
  • Unit 3: 4.4 - 4.0 = 0.4
  • Total profits = 0.9

We are interested to see if Bidder A could decrease her first unit bids in order to still win 3 units but increase her profits on winning the first unit. Table 4 illustrates this case when Bidder A changes her first unit bid from 7.0 to 6.0 while keeping her bid on the second and third unit constant. Bidder A still wins 3 units but is no longer the highest bidder - she is now the 3rd, 4th and 8th highest bidder - a ranking which is essentially meaningless as all that matters is that she is among the 8 highest bids. Thus in the case of Table 4, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as:

  • Unit 1: 7.3 - 6.0 = 1.3
  • Unit 2 : 6.0 - 5.8 = 0.2
  • Unit 3: 4.4 - 4.0 = 0.4
  • Total profits = 1.9

To summarize, examples 1 and 2 illustrated the effect of Bidder A using different strategies on total profits. As was the case in example 1, Bidder A earned more profit when she won 3 units compared to the case when she won 3 unit. Example 2 illustrates another scenario when Bidder A actually earned more revenue by lowering her bid on her first unit while keeping her bid on the second and third units constant. Note, it is never profitable for Bidder A to increase her first bid above her valuation for a given unit due to the fact that she will necessarily earn negative profit by paying more for a good than what she values it.
