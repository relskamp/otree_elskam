The following examples illustrate the pay-your-bid pricing rule and how profits are calculated. Each example will be illustrated from the perspective of bidder A who is competing against two rival bidders B and C. Each of the three bidders with demand for three units are competing in one auction in which there are eight units of the same good to be auctioned.

Example 1. First, we focus on Table 1 in Example 1. Note bids have been sorted from highest to lowest, values are shown next to the bids. The 9th losing bid has been highlighted with a pink background. The 8 highest bids above this pink background each earn an item. In the example depicted in Table 1, Bidder A earned 2 unit while bidder B and C both earned 3 units. Thus in the case of Table 1, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: 73 - 70 = 3
  • Unit 2: 63 - 58 = 5
  • and nothing on unit 3
  • Total profit = 8

In this example, Bidder A could have earned a third unit by bidding more, provided that bid was sufficiently high to edge out Bidder C. Table 2 illustrates the case in which Bidder A changes her bid on her third unit from 20 to 40; and all other bids are held constant. Focusing on Table 2, we see that Bidder A wins 3 units and bidder B wins 3 units while bidder C now only wins 2. Thus in the case of Table 2, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: 73 - 70 = 3
  • Unit 2: 63 - 58 = 5
  • Units 3: 44 - 40 = 4
  • Total profits = 12

In comparing the auction outcomes in Table 1 and Table 2, we see that Bidder A was able to increase her profits by bidding more on her third unit in order to win 3 units rather than 2.

Focusing on Table 3, we see that Bidder A earned 3 unit while bidder B earned 3 units and Bidder C won only 2 units. Profits for bidder A on this unit are calculated as follows: the valuation of the unit less the market price.

Thus in the case of Table 3, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as:

  • Unit 1: 73 - 70 = 3
  • Unit 2 : 60 - 58 = 2
  • Unit 3: 44 - 40 = 4
  • Total profits = 9

We are interested to see if Bidder A could decrease her first unit bids in order to still win 3 units but increase her profits on winning the first unit. Table 4 illustrates this case when Bidder A changes her first unit bid from 70 to 60 while keeping her bid on the second and third unit constant. Bidder A still wins 3 units but is no longer the highest bidder - she is now the 3rd, 4th and 8th highest bidder - a ranking which is essentially meaningless as all that matters is that she is among the 8 highest bids. Thus in the case of Table 4, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as:

  • Unit 1: 73 - 60 = 13
  • Unit 2 : 60 - 58 = 2
  • Unit 3: 44 - 40 = 4
  • Total profits = 19

To summarize, examples 1 and 2 illustrated the effect of Bidder A using different strategies on total profits. As was the case in example 1, Bidder A earned more profit when she won 3 units compared to the case when she won 3 unit. Example 2 illustrates another scenario when Bidder A actually earned more revenue by lowering her bid on her first unit while keeping her bid on the second and third units constant. Note, it is never profitable for Bidder A to increase her bid her valuation due to the fact that she will necessarily earn negative profit by paying more for a good than what she values it.
