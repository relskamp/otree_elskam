Example 1. First, we focus on Table 1. Note Bidder A obtains 1 unit while Bidder B obtains 2 units. Thus in the case of Table 1, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: $63 - $58 = $5
  • and nothing on unit 2
  • Total profit = $5

In this example, Bidder A could have obtained a second unit by bidding more, provided that bid was sufficiently high to edge out Bidder B. Table 2 illustrates the case in which Bidder A changes her bid on her second unit from $20 to $40; and all other bids are held constant. Focusing on Table 2, we see that Bidder A purchases two units while Bidder B now only purchases one. Thus in the case of Table 2, Bidder A’s profits are calculated as

  • Unit 1: $63 - $58 = $5
  • Units 2: $44 - $40 = $4
  • Total profits = $9

In comparing the auction outcomes in Table 1 and Table 2, we see that Bidder A was able to increase her profits by bidding more on her second unit in order to purchase two units rather than one.
